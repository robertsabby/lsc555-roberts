Evaluation of the Huntington Digital Library Website

“The Huntington invites all on a journey of discovery, insight, and connection.”

Introduction

The Huntington is a library, botanical garden, and art museum founded in 1919 by Henry E. and Arabella Huntington. It is located in San Marino, California. The library contains 12 million items from as early as the 11th centuries up until the present day. The collection includes books, manuscripts, photographs, historical records, and maps. These different types of material are all included in the digital collection. Much, but not all, of the collection is available online in the Huntington Digital Library. The digital library is free and open to the public. It is a basic website that is easy to use for people of all technological skill levels.

About the Digital Library

The target audience for the Huntington Digital Library is made up of four groups:
  • Researchers: Prospective researchers might use the Digital Library's Catalogue to search for materials they need for their research when evaluating whether to visit the Huntington Library. Some researchers might find the resources they need from the library are digitized, so they can use the website to conduct all of their research.
  • Applicants for fellowships: The Huntington Library offers fellowships and prospective applicants might use the Online Catalogue and Digital Library to assess whether they wish to apply for a fellowship.
  • Potential visitors: Potential visitors might use the Digital Library and Online Catalogue to plan their visit.
  • History and Literature Enthusiasts: The Digital Library is a great resource for people interested in history, art, and literature who would like to see items in the collection.

The Huntington Library contains 11 million items in total, but only a “fraction” available online. The digital collection includes books, manuscripts, photographs, historical records, and maps. The Digital Library does not highlight exactly how many items in the collection have been digitized and made available online. The Digital Library offers features for visitors including a "Virtual Reading Room," a "Frequently Asked Questions" page, and the option to "Ask a Librarian" via an online form.

A catalogue is available online, but the full collection is not represented in the catalogue (a guide to what is and is not online is available on website).

The online catalogue is searchable by:
  • Keyword
  • Author
  • Title
  • Author & Title
  • Subject
  • Form/Genre
  • LC Call Number
  • Place of Publication
  • Standard Number

Pros & Cons

The Digital Library's website offers users a readable, navigable, and straightforward experience. Some pros include navigation/quick links available from a drop-down menu and a search bar at the top of the homepage for users who are looking for specific material. The advanced search bar, in particular, offers a useful tool for prospective researchers who are looking for specific items in the collection. The digital website presents highlights of the collection on the homepage. These highlights are mainly organized by subject and the highlights include a variety of items in the collection (manuscripts, photos, maps, etc.). The highlights of the collection are helpful for users who are not looking for anything specific but would like to browse the collection. The collection highlights on the homepage are also convenient for users who have an idea of what they are looking for (maps, for example) but are not looking for any specific items and therefore need a quick link to maps, but not an advanced search function. There are also options for users who would like to explore the digital collection in a more systematic way. There are links to “Library Collections” highlighting the collection’s strengths and the Huntington Library Catalogue in the top half of the home page.

However, the Digital Library's website is lacking pizazz and could be better organized and more user friendly. First, the introductory text is in small font and could be more dynamic and readable. Second, the introductory text includes important links that might be lost/hard to find in the paragraphs of small text. Third, navigation could also be better. Although the page has a search bar and a drop down menu, the drop down menu is hard to find as it is found in the upper right hand corner of the page. Fourth, the page is not visually exciting. The collection highlights are in a stable grid format which is not visually engaging. The website does not support any audio, visual, or interactive features on the homepage. Instead, it offers just text, pictures, and hyperlinks. Finally, some of the language on the page might be confusing to users. For example, there are links to the “Library Collections” and “Collection Guides” but it might not be clear to the user what the difference between those tools actually are.

Suggestions for Improvement

The website of the Huntington Digital Library would benefit from better organization and more visual interest. The Digital Library would greatly benefit from a navigation bar at the top of the page for easy navigation. That would make the important links easy to find wherever the user is on the website. It would also create more visual distinction between navigation and collection highlights. This navigation bar would replace the drop down menu that currently exists, but the advanced search bar should remain at the top of the screen as part of the new navigation bar. The “Huntington History,” “Collection Guides,” and “Building a Digital Library” pages are currently part of the grid with collection highlights. As they are background about the library itself, these pages should be highlighted separately from the grid with the collection highlights. As to the grid, the collection highlights should be highlighted in a more visually exciting way. A carousel would provide more visual interest and allow each collection highlight to be the focus of attention. A carousel would also mean that the pictures of items in the collection would be larger, which would be to the benefit of the collection items themselves. This way the items highlighted from the collection would be larger, easier to see, and altogether more attractive for users of the website.
